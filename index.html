Historical Context

A Transitional
Assertion of Self

Adopted by the Supreme Soviet of the Kazakh SSR on October 25, 1990, the Declaration of State Sovereignty marked a pivotal step in the nation's self-determination — yet it did not sever ties with the Soviet Union. Rather, it established the supremacy of Kazakh laws over all-Union legislation within the republic's territory.

The document proclaimed ownership of natural resources, land, and subsoil — a critical economic assertion that laid the groundwork for fiscal independence. It declared Kazakhstan's right to maintain its own citizenship, banking system, and foreign economic relations, while still acknowledging the existence of the Union framework.

"The Declaration was not a break — it was a negotiation. Kazakhstan sought to redefine its relationship with Moscow, not eliminate it. Sovereignty was claimed, but within a federal envelope."

— Constitutionalist analysis, modern scholarship

vs
Full Sovereignty

The Complete
Break from the Union

On December 16, 1991 — just days before the formal dissolution of the USSR — the Supreme Soviet adopted the Constitutional Law on State Independence of the Republic of Kazakhstan. This was not a declaration but a law: binding, structural, and absolute.

The law definitively proclaimed Kazakhstan a fully independent state, establishing its own borders as inviolable, creating the foundations for a national armed forces, and affirming the right to pursue an independent foreign policy. All Union-level authority was extinguished within Kazakh territory.

"The 1991 law transformed aspirations into architecture. Where the Declaration spoke of priority, the Independence Law spoke of finality. Kazakhstan was no longer a republic within a union — it was a nation among nations."

— Post-Soviet constitutional law review

Part II

Constitutional Reforms

Objectives, Milestones, and Outcomes of Kazakhstan's constitutional development from 1993 to 2022

1993
First Constitution
🎯 Objective

Replace Soviet-era legal order with a sovereign constitutional framework. Establish the Republic of Kazakhstan as a unitary, democratic, secular, and social state founded on the rule of law.

🏛 Milestone

Adopted January 28, 1993 by the Supreme Soviet — Kazakhstan's first post-independence constitution. Introduced separation of powers, guaranteed fundamental rights, and defined state structure for the transitional period.

✓ Outcome

Created legal legitimacy for the new state, though its provisions proved insufficient for the rapid transition to a market economy. Lasted only two years before deep revision became necessary.

1995
Core Constitution
🎯 Objective

Overcome political deadlock between the executive and legislative branches. Establish a strong presidential system capable of driving economic reform and political stability during a volatile transition period.

🏛 Milestone

Adopted by national referendum on August 30, 1995 — National Constitution Day. Introduced a bicameral Parliament (Senate and Mazhilis), expanded presidential authority, and created the Constitutional Council.

✓ Outcome

Became the enduring constitutional backbone of Kazakhstan, providing a stable legal foundation for nearly three decades of economic and geopolitical development. Still in force today, albeit through significant amendments.

1998–2017
Incremental Reform
🎯 Objective

Gradually modernize the constitutional framework in response to political developments: extend presidential terms (1998), redistribute authority toward Parliament (2007), and begin institutional democratization (2017).

🏛 Milestone

Three rounds of amendments: 1998 extended the presidential term to 7 years; 2007 introduced party-list elections and enhanced parliamentary oversight; 2017 transferred significant executive functions to the government and Parliament in a dual-track reform package.

✓ Outcome

A layered constitutional evolution that incrementally shifted the balance of power. The 2017 reforms in particular set the stage for the political transition that followed in 2019, signaling readiness for a new era of governance.

2022
New Kazakhstan
🎯 Objective

Respond to the January 2022 civil unrest by fundamentally redesigning the state model — transitioning from a super-presidential system toward a more balanced distribution of power and a genuinely competitive political environment.

🏛 Milestone

June 5, 2022 constitutional referendum approved 56 amendments — the most comprehensive revision since 1995. Key changes: reduced presidential term to 7 non-renewable years, restored direct election of regional governors (akims), and re-established the Constitutional Court.

✓ Outcome

Marked the formal launch of President Tokayev's "New Kazakhstan" agenda, symbolizing a structural departure from the Nazarbayev era. Strengthened horizontal accountability and broadened civic participation mechanisms at local governance levels.
